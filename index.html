Quantitative Economics MW24.2

Experimental Economics

This course introduces students to experimental methods in economics, focusing on how controlled experiments can be used to test economic theories and inform policy decisions. The course begins with an introduction to the role of experiments in economics, discussing their historical development and comparison with other empirical methods. It then covers the principles of designing and conducting experiments, emphasizing key concepts such as internal and external validity, incentive structures, and behavioral biases. Students will engage with foundational and contemporary research in experimental economics to understand how laboratory and field experiments contribute to economic knowledge.

The second part of the course delves into key experimental topics, including individual decision-making under risk and uncertainty, bargaining and coordination games, public goods and cooperation dilemmas, and market experiments. Students will examine seminal studies on judgment biases, social preferences, and strategic interactions. A special focus is given to auctions and market behavior, where experimental methods have provided crucial insights into economic mechanisms such as bidding strategies and price formation. The course also introduces recent advances in behavioral economics, including the intersection of psychology and economics in shaping human decision-making.

Course requirements and grading

Participants should have a solid background and interest in empirical work and statistics, with (at least) basic knowledge of the linear regression model (OLS). Grading will be based on:

  • small group presentation (20% of final grade) + constructive comments during tutorials (10%)
  • final exam on July 18, 2025 (70% of final grade)

To pass, you have to participate actively and constructively in at least 70% sessions. If you miss a session due to health reasons, send me a certificate from your GP within three days.
